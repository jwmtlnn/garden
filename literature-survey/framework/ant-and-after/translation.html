Introduction & general definition

Rather than viewing the social as a separate domain, ANT sees it as an effect of heterogeneous associations (Latour, 2005). Within ANT, the concept of translation is central to understanding how these associations are formed and maintained. Developed by Michel Serres and then articulated by Michel Callon, translation emphasizes the continuity of displacements and transformations that occur within a project, story, case etc. - a process (Callon, 1986).

Four Moments of Translation, after (Callon, 1986)

1. Problematization
Initial attempt by an actor to define a problem and to position other actors in relation to it.

2. Interessement
Attempts to persuade other actors to accept the definitions and roles proposed in the problematization.

3. Enrolment
Actors negotiate and define their roles within the network, establishing stable relationships.

4. Mobilization
Ensuring that designated spokespersons effectively represent their constituencies.

Initially the semiotic relations were "left open", but contemporarily ANT highlights how links are made between different entities, focusing on the work of making two things that are not the same, equivalent (Law, 1999)p.8). So it is not just a linguistic phenomenon but also a semiotic operation involving the displacement of logical relations between seemingly opposed sets of significations, interests, and concerns (Callon, 1980)p.211);(Brown & Capdevila, 1999)p.32).

Within this process of translation, the identification of obligatory passage points (OPP) plays a crucial role. They are specific "points" in this program through which the interests of all involved actors must pass to be satisfied. (Callon, 1986)p.205-206.) Somewhat related concept discussed e.g. by (Latour & Woolgar, 1986) is "black-boxing" or punctualisation, where the successful translation leads to the network being represented and treated as a single, coherent entity - as a black box.

Translation vs. Diffusion (Transportation)

The diffusion model is a way of understanding how facts, artefacts etc. spread in time and space, that ANT critiques as being overly simplistic. The diffusion model suggests that once a fact or artifact is established, people easily agree to transmit it, as if the object itself forces their assent. This model often invents a form of technological determinism, where innovations seem to spread with their own inherent strength, irresistibly imposing themselves, as if having their own vis inertia and explaining non-adoption as mere "resistance". Contrarily, innovation seen through translation (Model of Interessement) emphasizes that an innovation's journey involves active work in shaping and building a network of heterogenous relationships. The interests of different actors (human and nonhuman) are negotiated and potentially transformed in relation to the innovation, an the innovation itself may very well be transformed as it moves through different contexts and encounters different actors. (Latour, 1987)p.132-144.)
