Actor-Network Theory

Quote

... an approach to sociotechnical analysis that treats entities and materialities as enacted and relational effects, and explores the configuration and reconfiguration of those relations (Law, 2004)p.157).

Introduction & general definition

Actor-Network Theory (ANT, fin. toimijaverkkoteoria) is often described as an approach, sensibility, program or orientation to relationality, materiality, heterogeneity and process for sociotechnical or -material analysis (Law, 2004)p.157);(Latour, 1999). It aims for empirical descriptions of the relational ties and sociomaterial flux and it's translations in a network. (Latour, 2005). The unit of analysis is often process (Miettinen, 1999)p.174). Despite its name, whether it truly is a theory is debatable and there is even an a-theoretical nature to it (Latour, 2005).

Historical context

ANT has its roots in the Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation (CSI) of the Mines de Paris and emerged from the field of Science and Technology Studies (STS) in the early 1980s. It was/is often referred also to as "a sociology of translations" or "a sociology of associations" before the term Actor-Network Theory was coined. (Akrich, 2023);(Callon, 2001). Foundational scholars are considered to be Bruno Latour, Madeleine Akrich, John Law and Michel Callon. Other influential, but not at all peripheral, are e.g. Annemarie Mol, Steve Woolgar, Karin Knorr-Cetina and Donna Haraway. In the context of innovation systems, has Reijo Miettinen examined the applicability of ANT since the 1990s. As previously stated, it is well used, developed and debated in social sciences, but has been "spread" over various other fields and uses (Law, 1999)p.10)

Established definitions

ANT has evolved through the decades and because it lacks a theoretical unity it might be difficult to define (Miettinen, 1999)p.171). There is no generic way to apply ANT because there is no clear "methodological prescription", but there are many key elements and choices that persist in it's intellectual practice (Jackson, 2015) or show up in contemporary ANT "and After".

Note

Actor-Network Theory views reality as constructed through dynamic networks of diverse actors, both human and non-human, treating them with generalized symmetry and ontological agnosticism. These actor-networks, characterized by heterogeneity and hybridity, are enacted through processes of translation, where e.g. inscriptions and quasi-objects facilitate interactions via intermediaries and mediators. Through enrolment, actants are integrated, and often networks have obligatory passage points where all actors must converge. Immutable mobiles and objects stabilize and disseminate influence, revealing the multiplicity inherent in social and material realities. Complex networks are often simplified into punctualised black boxes, which the ANT tries to provide a view into.

ANT does not use "society" or "social" as explanations in its analysis, but instead tries to explain them "in the making" as an ongoing achievement. It aims not to reproduce the classical oppositions or dualisms of nature/culture, micro/macro, agency/culture etc. or to see facts mirroring nature, but to show how they are constructed and to provide tools for analyzing that process. (Callon, 2001). It is thus a constructionist, post-structuralist, material-semiotic and an anti-essentialist approach. In the context of innovation processes, ANT is commonly used to study them as a simultaneous development of an artefact and a network of actors connected to it (Miettinen, 1999)p.170).

ANT and After; Near ANT; methodology

Methodological principles (Bignetti et al., 2023);(Latour, 2005);(Law, 2004)
  • Follow the actors
  • Radical empiricism
  • No pre-defined social
  • Focus on associations
  • Translation, not diffusion
  • Description, not explanation first
  • Symmetry principle
  • Non-Euclideanity

Adjacent approaches

In many ways a similar approach called the Social Construction of Technology (SCOT, foundational authors being Wiebe Bijker & Trevor Pinch) can not be accounted as ANT, according to (Latour, 2005)p. 10-11), since in it the "the social is kept stable all along and accounts for the shape of technological change." Another similarly kept approach is the Cultural Historical Activity Theory (AT or CHAT), which has a different disciplinary and philosophical background, but shares many methodological grounds (Miettinen, 1999). A closely related and broader body of thought is New Materialism, which it shares with e.g. the "flat" ontology and in some sense ANT (and perhaps also AT) can be seen as a contribution to it (Kamp, 2019).

Critical engagement

Although ANT is historically based in the sociology of science and technology, according to (Miettinen, 1999)p.181) the ANT task for innovation processes "seems hopeless".
