Innovation System

Introduction & general definition

Innovation Systems is a concept in understanding how new, economically useful knowledge is produced, diffused, and utilized within an economy. This concept moves beyond viewing innovation as isolated events, emphasizing it as an ongoing, ubiquitous, and cumulative process that is deeply rooted to learning in the economic structure and institutional setup. (Lundvall, 1992)(Johnson, 1992). The term is considered a concept rather than a general theory, though it is built upon a series of coherent assumptions derived from systematic empirical work (Lundvall, 2010b)

Established definitions

Innovation systems can be understood in a narrow sense, which includes organizations directly involved in research and exploration like R&D departments, technological institutes, and universities. In a broad sense, it includes all parts and aspects of the economic structure and institutional setup that affect learning, searching, and exploring, such as the production system, marketing system, and financial system. A National System of Innovation (NSI) specifically encompasses these elements and relationships located within or rooted inside the borders of a nation-state. (Freeman, 1992);(Lundvall, 1992).

Key concepts of National Systems of Innovation in (Lundvall, 2010a)
  • Institutions: formal organizations like firms, universities, government agencies and informal norms, routines, and rules that shape interactions.
  • Learning: innovation is fundamentally a learning process that occurs through interactions between actors in the system.
  • Interactions and Networks: relations between participants will be influenced by the specific characteristics of the partners in a number of dimensions.
  • Differentiation of Innovation: The traditional, broad term is differentiated to distinguish between "incremental innovations" and "radical innovations".
  • Development Blocks: drawing on Dahmén's idea, refers to interconnected sequences of innovations and investments in related sectors that form a dynamic process of economic development.

Critical engagement

The term has been introduced from research into politics. In politics and policy it has been recognized to work as a loose border object rather than well defined and explanatory scientific term, in a similar way that Porters ideas have been used in policymaking. Despite attempts in "mainstream" and "non-mainstream" economics (Dalum, 1992);(Fagerberg, 1992), it seems that innovation systems cannot be quantitatively compared in a fruitful way: they have to be situated and can't be generalized very well. This makes case studies an important way to gain understanding of innovation systems. (Miettinen, 2002).
